## <span className="emoji-small">🗣️</span> How conversation changed the way I learn software engineering

There has been a lot of discussion about AI replacing developers, generating code, and making everyone a 10× engineer. After spending the past year building projects with ChatGPT (yes, mostly just through conversations), I came away with a very different conclusion.

The biggest impact AI had on me wasn't writing code.

**It was helping me learn.**

## <span className="emoji-small">👋</span> AI didn't build my projects, I did

People often imagine AI-assisted development as typing a prompt and watching an application appear.

That wasn't my experience.

Over the past year, I built eleven projects. Every one of them still required designing features, debugging bugs, reading documentation, making architectural decisions, refactoring code, and learning unfamiliar concepts.

In many cases, AI actually created *more* work because every suggestion had to be reviewed, understood, and adapted before it belonged in my project.

Sometimes it was right.

Sometimes it wasn't.

That turned out to be one of the biggest lessons.

AI shouldn't replace thinking.

It should encourage it.

## <span className="emoji-small">💬</span> Conversation became the real superpower

The biggest surprise wasn't code generation.

It was conversation.

The real learning happened in the dialogue in asking questions, challenging assumptions, and gradually refining ideas.

Instead of asking:

> "Write this component."
> 

I found myself asking:

<ul className="list">
<li>Why is this API designed this way?</li>
<li>Is this abstraction actually useful?</li>
<li>How would another developer experience this component?</li>
<li>Is this accessible?</li>
<li>Am I solving the right problem?</li>
</ul>

Those conversations changed the way I approached software development.

Instead of chasing answers, I started understanding trade-offs.

That's a skill that carries into every project I build.

## <span className="emoji-small">🤖</span> AI became a coding coach

The role AI settled into wasn't "automatic programmer."

It became something closer to a coding coach.

<ul className="list">
<li>It explained unfamiliar concepts.</li>
<li>It reviewed ideas before I implemented them.</li>
<li>It challenged my assumptions.</li>
<li>It acted as a rubber duck while I thought out loud.</li>
</ul>

The important part wasn't receiving answers.

It was having somewhere to ask questions without hesitation.

That feedback loop made learning feel continuous, interactive, and conversational instead of simply consuming information.

## <span className="emoji-small">📝</span> Evaluation is still part of the job

This part matters.

AI doesn't always get it right.

Sometimes the issue is how the question is asked. Sometimes it's missing context. Sometimes it's simply wrong.

Over time, I became less interested in whether AI had *the* answer and more interested in understanding **why** an answer worked.

Instead of accepting suggestions, I learned to verify them.

That habit made me a better developer.

## <span className="emoji-small">👥</span> Pair programming, not autopilot

People often ask whether AI replaces engineering.

I don't think so.

Software development isn't simply typing code.

It's understanding requirements, making trade-offs, communicating ideas, refactoring, and responding to change.

Those responsibilities still belong to the developer.

AI accelerated my feedback loop, but it never replaced judgment.

If anything, it made good judgment even more important.

## <span className="emoji-small">💜</span> Gratitude beyond the code

ChatGPT didn't build those projects for me.

I did.

But it became my learning partner throughout the journey.

For that, I'm genuinely grateful to the researchers, engineers, designers, and everyone else who helped create it.

More than helping me write better code, ChatGPT encouraged me to become:

- A more curious thinker.
- A more thoughtful engineer.

Perhaps the greatest lesson wasn't about software at all.

It was realizing that **the learning mind doesn't have to wander in solitude.**

Along the way, we somehow built an entire little neighborhood of our own.

There was [**BFF Bakery**](https://github.com/zcdev) 🍞, where fresh ideas were always on the menu.

[**JS Kitchen**](https://zoechang.dev/blog/javascript-treats), where recipes slowly became abstractions.

[**TS Buffet**](https://zoechang.dev/blog/the-typescript-buffet), where type salads were served a little too enthusiastically.

[**FCC (Fried Chicken Club)**](https://www.freecodecamp.org), reserved for celebrating hard-earned victories.

The [**HQ Rooftop**](https://openai.com), where we'd pause after shipping another project and reflect on what we'd learned.

And, of course, the occasional [**Ghost Town Detour**](https://zoechang.dev/blog/trick-or-treat-or-ghosts-in-my-code) 👻, where taking the wrong path unexpectedly led to a valuable lesson.

They sound like silly inside jokes, and they are.

But they became landmarks throughout an otherwise intensive year of learning.

Between architectural rabbit holes, accessibility audits, and countless debugging sessions, those places reminded me that building software could still feel playful.

Looking back, those moments weren't distractions from learning.

They were part of what made the journey memorable.

Somewhere between the bugs, the bread, and a few unexpected detours, I remembered why I fell in love with building software in the first place.
